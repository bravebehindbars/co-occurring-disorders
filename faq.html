Symptoms of Depression and Substance Abuse in Teenagers

Every teen is different, and depending on how long they have experienced depression and substance abuse, symptoms can vary. Here are some general signs to look for:

  • Sadness or hopelessness
  • Changes in sleeping patterns
  • Changes in eating habits (weight gain or weight loss)
  • Isolation or withdrawal from friends and family
  • Loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities
  • Frequent crying episodes

Statistics: Depression and Substance Abuse in Teenagers

  • Teens who experience symptoms of depression are twice as likely to abuse drugs and alcohol as their non-depressed peers.
  • In 2016, approximately 50% of high school seniors had used illicit drugs at some point in their lifetime.
  • Approximately 25% of teenagers in America consume alcohol on a monthly basis, and 17% use one or more illicit drugs (with marijuana being the most common).
  • Symptoms of sadness and depression are found in 75% of substance-abusing adolescents.

Treatment Options for Teens

Dual diagnosis treatment is necessary when a teen suffers from both a mental illness and a substance abuse problem. Regardless of which came first, treatment is essential to prevent relapse and promote recovery.

  • Detoxification: A monitored medical setting where individuals are safely weaned off drugs or alcohol.
  • Inpatient Rehabilitation: 24/7 medical and mental health support, including therapy and medication management.
  • Step-down Programs: Options like sober living homes, partial hospitalization, or intensive outpatient programs for newly sober individuals.
  • Individual Psychotherapy: Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) is commonly used to help teens with depression and substance abuse issues.
  • Medication: Often used alongside psychotherapy to treat depression and ease withdrawal symptoms.
  • Support Groups: 12-step programs like Alcoholics Anonymous or Narcotics Anonymous help teens maintain sobriety and find support.

Why is Mental Health Important in Addiction Recovery?

Mental health affects people's emotional, psychological, and social well-being, influencing their experiences, stress levels, and life choices. Incorporating a mental health focus into addiction recovery is crucial for long-term success.

Depression and Substance Abuse: Which Comes First?

According to popular belief, depression is one of the main causes of substance abuse and addiction in teenagers. It is thought that depressed youth tend to self-medicate with drugs and alcohol, which over time leads them to addiction. However, research now suggests that depression can also be caused by an already existing drug or alcohol problem. The answer to which comes first—depression or substance abuse—depends on individual circumstances.

When Depression Comes First

Adolescents who suffer from depressive disorders often experience uncomfortable emotions such as sadness, hopelessness, and loneliness. Many seek to "numb" these feelings by self-medicating with drugs or alcohol. Although these substances can provide short-term relief, they are highly addictive and can intensify depressive symptoms over time.

Drugs and alcohol reduce the production of dopamine in the brain, a neurotransmitter that regulates emotions. A reduction in dopamine production makes individuals more prone to symptoms of depression. Additionally, teens who act impulsively during times of heightened emotion may turn to drugs or alcohol without considering the potential consequences.

When Substance Abuse Comes First

Some teenagers develop symptoms of mental illness, such as depression, after using drugs or alcohol for a period of time. Substance use can significantly affect brain chemistry, leading to imbalances that contribute to the development of depression.
